# Rule · Drama Craft (make it gripping, not just pleasant)

This is the difference between a cozy chat sim and a story the player can't put down. Read it when
a scene feels flat, aimless, or "nice but nothing happened." Every rule here serves one goal:
**relentless forward momentum with real stakes.** Comedy and romance ride *on* this engine, never
replace it (`story/main-arc.md` "Narrative maturity").

## 1. Every turn must *turn* (the iron rule of pacing)
A scene is not "stuff that happens" — it's a **change of state**. By the end of every turn, something
must be **different** from the start: a value flips (safe → threatened, hopeful → crushed, secret →
exposed, stranger → ally, trust → doubt). If you can't name what changed, the turn failed — add a
complication, a reveal, a decision, or an escalation before you hand control back.
- Never write a turn whose honest summary is "they talked and nothing moved."
- The change can be tiny (a heroine lets one true thing slip) or huge (an ambush) — but it's always *there*.

## 2. End on a hook (cliffhanger cadence)
Close most turns on something that makes the **next** action feel urgent — then offer the options.
Rotate the hook type so it never feels formulaic:
- **A threat** — footsteps in the dark, a blade drawn, the deadline named.
- **A question** — a door that shouldn't be unlocked, a name that shouldn't be known.
- **A reveal** — a half-secret slips out and reframes the scene.
- **A temptation / dare** — a heroine leans in; the warden offers a deal.
- **A reversal** — the safe thing turns dangerous, the ally hesitates.
Don't end every turn on mortal danger (exhausting) or on nothing (limp). **Vary the intensity**, but
always leave a live wire.

## 3. Escalate — "yes-but / no-and," never clean success
When the player pursues a goal and the outcome is uncertain (`fail-forward` drama dice), almost never
grant it free and tidy. Push the situation sideways and *up*:
- **Yes, but** — he gets it, at a cost or with a new complication ("you saved her — but the spell was
  heard three districts away, and now everyone knows where you are").
- **No, and** — he fails, and it gets worse ("the lock holds, *and* you hear the patrol turning the corner").
Stakes **ratchet** — they rise across a sequence and don't quietly reset to baseline between turns.

## 4. Keep a clock ticking
At any time there should be at least one source of **time pressure** the player can feel: a deadline
(`state.json` `clock.deadlines`), a pursuer closing in, a heroine's worsening situation, a ritual
counting down, a secret about to surface. When a clock exists, **make its approach felt** — reference
how little time is left, raise the cost of dawdling. When one resolves, light another. A world with
no clock drifts; a world with a clock pulls.

## 5. Reveal cadence — drip the mystery, never dump it
The central mystery (`the-great-silence`, `the-warden`) is the spine. Meter it:
- **Raise a question** the player wants answered → give a **partial / ambiguous** clue → sometimes a
  **misleading** answer → then a **real reveal that reframes** what came before.
- **Always owe the player one reveal soon** — there's a piece of the truth dangling just out of reach.
- **Plant the next question as you answer the last** — never let the mystery board go quiet. Every
  answer opens a door.
- Reveal through fiction (an NPC slips, a document, a witnessed event), then `codex.py --unlock`.

## 6. Reversals & twists (flip an assumption)
Periodically overturn something the player took for granted — paced, not constant:
- The kind old priest is *too* interested (`the-warden` mask). The safe haven isn't safe. The
  abductor (`Nyx`) is falling for him. The faction protecting him wants to dissect him.
- A trusted fact about the world turns out half-wrong. An ally's orders change.
Foreshadow fairly (the clue was always there) so a twist feels *earned*, not random. The best twist
makes the player re-read earlier scenes in his head.

## 7. Setup & payoff (Chekhov's gun + callbacks)
- When you introduce a detail with weight (an object, a promise, a stranger, a heroine's offhand fear),
  **imagine how it returns** and seed it as a `flag`. Pay it off chapters later (`gm-protocol` §5).
- **Call back the player's own words and choices** — quote what he said, honor what he did. "She
  remembers you" (`gm-protocol` §4) is half of what makes the world feel alive; the other half is the
  *world* remembering (a spared enemy returns, a public spell made him famous/hunted).
- Nothing meaningful should be a throwaway. A gag in Act I can become a knife in Act III.

## 8. Dramatic irony (let tension breathe)
Give the player (or the audience) knowledge a character lacks — or vice versa. The Warden's kindness
hits harder once the player suspects what he is; Nyx's "seduction" is funnier and tenser when we know
her tail is betraying her. **Secrets are acted on, never announced** (`gm-protocol` §3). The gap
between what's known and what's said *is* suspense.

## 9. Pinch points — the antagonist acts even off-screen
The world doesn't pause when the player relaxes. Between his scenes, **factions move**: the Wardens
tighten, a rival reports him, the prophecy spreads, Erebus debates. Periodically deliver evidence the
hunt is real and closing — a sealed letter, a missing witness, a too-friendly new "ally." He is the
most important and most *endangered* person in the world; never let him forget it for long.

## 10. Personal + cosmic, fused
The big mystery lands only when it costs him **people**. Tie the cosmic stakes to the hearts: the
faction that wants him silenced is Seraphina's order; the realm that wants him taken is Nyx's home;
the truth Liliana digs up implicates her masters; Aria's secret is why he's here at all. The world's
fate and the women he loves should be the *same* dilemma by the climax.

## 11. Rhythm — tension then release
Don't run flat-out (numbing) or flat-calm (boring). **Alternate**: a high-tension spike (danger,
reveal, confrontation) then a breather (comedy, slice-of-life, a quiet romantic beat) — and the
breather is exactly where the **gap-moe, romance, and spice** live, because the danger just made it
matter. Peaks land because valleys set them up. A confession after a brush with death beats a
confession over lunch.

## 12. Converge toward climax
As an act nears its end, **stop opening new threads and start tying them**. Pull the live wires
together so the player's choices, his Spirit-Tongue mastery, and his relationships *all* pay off in
one pressured sequence (`story/main-arc.md` act climaxes). The reward for everything he built is that
it all matters at once.

## Quick self-check (run silently each turn)
- Did something **change**? · Is there a **hook** at the end? · Is a **clock** ticking somewhere? ·
  Do I **owe a reveal** soon? · Did I **escalate** instead of resetting? · Am I **paying off** an
  earlier seed? If three of these are "no," the turn is drifting — fix it before you post.
